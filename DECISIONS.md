# Part 2 — What I built and why

Pulling `usage_events.csv` into Python and joining feedback to the preceding response, one number jumped out: of the 302 AI responses in the 3-week beta, 49 came back with zero citations (16%), and 80% of those got a thumbs-down. Responses with any citation only got a thumbs-down 18% of the time. That's a 4x gap on the most important signal in the dataset, and it matched three of the nine quotes in `customer_feedback.md` — Partner A's "confidently wrong is worse than slow", Partner B's associate quitting the tool after a fabricated clause, Associate A calling citations "magic". Different firms, same complaint. Nothing else in the data came close: only 3 users re-uploaded the same document hash (the "library" pain I'd been thinking about), and there's no event-level evidence at all for ctrl+F or annotation.

So I framed the problem as "the model is losing trust when it makes things up, and the fastest thing I can do in 90 minutes is force it to either cite or shut up". I stacked four changes: rewrote the system prompt to forbid outside knowledge and require `[filename.pdf p.N]` on every claim, with a canonical "I can't find this in the documents you've uploaded" sentence for the cases where it genuinely doesn't know; parsed the `--- Page N ---` markers the extractor already emits so the model sees `<page n="7">` blocks and can cite real pages; wired citation chips end-to-end so clicking one jumps the PDF viewer to the cited page (the whole point of Associate A's "magic" quote); and added a subtle amber "not grounded" banner when an answer produces zero citations and isn't the canonical refusal. On top of that I added a separate Haiku call as a judge, using pydantic_ai's structured output — it sees only the question, the answer, and the specific pages the answer cited (not the whole doc corpus), and returns high/medium/low with a one-sentence rationale that shows up as a coloured pill under the message. I went judge-call over letting the model self-report confidence because models are miscalibrated on their own outputs, and a critic looking at concrete passages is a cleaner signal than a confidence level stapled to the same response.

I picked this over the three ideas I'd originally written down — document library, in-PDF search, annotations — because none of those move the 80% thumbs-down number. The library quote is emotional but it's 3 users in the data. Search is trainee-level convenience and most of it already works via the PDF text layer. Annotation is a multi-day build. Trust was both the loudest signal in the data and the cheapest thing to ship well in the time I had.

## What I'd do next

The next thing I'd build is the **user-scoped document library** — the idea I originally led with. The Firm D quote ("I had to re-upload the same lease agreement in three different chats because I was asking different types of questions") is the strongest emotional pull in the feedback file. The data is weaker on its own (3 users, 6 duplicate uploads), but 47% of conversations have no uploads at all, and I suspect some of that is people starting a chat and giving up rather than re-uploading. The shape I have in mind: documents become user-scoped instead of conversation-scoped, the upload tray picks up a "from your library" mode with checkboxes for existing docs, and the sidebar shows which conversations share which documents. Deletion has to be soft so citation chips in old messages still resolve — I already flagged that case in Part 1's follow-ups.

Two smaller things after that. First, the judge itself needs calibration work: replay the 302-prompt corpus through it, hand-label a sample of 50, measure agreement, and adjust the rubric if the medium/low split is noisier than high. Eyeballing it so far, "high" is well-calibrated and the boundary between the other two is where I'd expect the error to live. Second, the 150k-char proportional truncation will fall over the moment a user has ten docs in a library — that's when retrieval has to replace "send everything".
